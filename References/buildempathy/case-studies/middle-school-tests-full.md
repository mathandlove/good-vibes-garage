# Motivating Middle School Students to Take Tests

**Source URL:** https://www.buildempathy.com/casestudies/middleschooltests  
**Scraped:** April 2026

**Team:** Meredith Wilson, Christine Zanchi, Elliott Hedman

---

## Problem

Students were not taking diagnostics seriously, answering questions randomly. These false efforts were affecting school scores and students were being placed in content that was too easy for them.

---

## Outcome

The team prototyped different messages to motivate students to try harder on tests. Middle school students paid the most attention to high school students who adults didn't believe in. Students reported more desire to try hard when exposed to student-led messaging.

---

## Key Findings

### High School Students Command Attention
- **Before:** Entertaining animations gave instructions that students didn't believe in or care about.
- **After:** Real student stories captured attention. Middle schoolers aspire to be high schoolers and care deeply about their perspectives. The recommendation was to conduct all messaging through a high school student.

### Emphasize "Prove Yourself"
- **Before:** Initial messaging downplayed challenge with "you can do this" and "this will be easy."
- **After:** Students responded better to messaging about "I don't give up" and "proving yourself." Students wanted messaging that believed in their capabilities.

### Address Anxiety, Not Motivation
- **Before:** Messaging emphasized "Try Hard."
- **After:** Using eye-tracking and MOXO sensors, researchers discovered students quit after encountering difficult problems. The revised approach emphasized "Getting Something Wrong is Okay" and noted some questions are challenging even for most 8th graders.

---

## Key Insights
- "If the narrator doesn't believe in the student, the student does not trust them."
- "If you say 'this test will be easy' students will think you don't know them or think they are dumb."
- "Proving a teacher wrong, that you can do it, is a highly engaging and motivating archetype."
- "Proving to a teacher that they underestimated you is highly motivating."
- High school student videos were significantly more engaging than other formats.
- Student stories were the most remembered messaging elements.
- Students were fast-guessing to avoid anxiety, not due to apathy.
