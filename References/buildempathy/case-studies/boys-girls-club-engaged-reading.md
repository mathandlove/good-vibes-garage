# Boys & Girls Club — Engaged Reading (Reluctant Readers)

**Source URL:** https://www.buildempathy.com/casestudies/engagedreading  
**Client:** Boys & Girls Club  
**Objective:** Design reading lessons for reluctant 3rd and 4th graders that encourage active engagement and repeated participation

---

## Team
Garrett Hedman, Meredith Wilson, Christine Zanchi, Elysa Greenberger, Sara Stump

---

## Key Outcome

Through iterative prototyping, the team successfully transformed reading lessons so students "actively read each line, pay attention to feedback, and ask to do it again" — contrasting sharply with initial prototypes where many refused to complete a single paragraph.

---

## Three Core Design Changes

### 1. Add "Weight" to Questions
**Problem:** Students showed indifference to incorrect answers and skipped grades entirely.

**Solution:** Implemented a coin-reward system — students earned one coin per correct answer within two attempts. This framework motivated careful re-reading and thoughtful problem-solving, with later research confirming coins correlated strongly with achievement.

### 2. Remove Visual Clutter
**Problem:** Eye-tracking revealed students bypassed text to focus on more visually engaging questions and images.

**Solution:** Streamlined interface displaying one to two sentences at a time, with questions and images appearing only after text completion. Design inspired by the app Hooked — helped students focus and "ease into reading."

### 3. Bring in Big Questions
**Problem:** Students read solely to finish quickly without genuine engagement or enjoyment.

**Solution:** Introduced "Wonder Questions" at lesson starts (e.g., "Why do vultures eat dead animals?"), followed by peer discussion. This approach instilled purpose and curiosity throughout reading activities.

---

## Additional Research Findings

- **Peer interaction:** Students engaged faster and answered more readily through conversation than typing
- **Skip alerts:** Children appreciated notifications about skipped reading despite initial concern
- **Cartoon illustrations:** Simple graphics similar to "Diary of a Wimpy Kid" aided comprehension without overwhelming students
- **Question quality:** Effective Wonder Questions require genuine uncertainty — adult-solvable questions lack engagement value
- **Game timing:** Pre-reading games proved more effective at engagement than post-reading rewards
