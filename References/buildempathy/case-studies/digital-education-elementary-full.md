# Mapping the Emotional Experience of Digital Education (Elementary)

**Source URL:** https://www.buildempathy.com/casestudies/digitalelementary  
**Scraped:** April 2026

**Team:** Garrett Hedman, Meredith Wilson, Christine Zanchi, Elysa Greenberger, Sara Stump

---

## Overview

mPath conducted research at three Title 1 schools observing digital learning software usage, plus home observations of children playing video games to inform product development.

**Key Outcome:** Research shaped prototyping decisions regarding reading, rewards, and framing.

---

## Major Findings

### 1. Erase the Line Between Game and Learning
- **Before:** Games functioned as rewards for academic performance; students avoided them and disengaged.
- **After:** When learning integrated directly into games, children requested more "math and reading" content and demonstrated heightened attention and accuracy.

### 2. Be Challenge-Centered
- **Before:** Students complained lessons were "baby lessons" — too simplistic, despite frequently answering incorrectly.
- **After:** Adding problem complexity created meaningful achievement. A six-month initiative focused on building "weight" in problems increased perceived accomplishment.

### 3. Lean In to Failure
- **Before:** Incorrect answers triggered immediate correction and progression to preserve confidence.
- **After:** Failure moments generated peak attention and help-seeking behavior. Offering guidance only after errors increased engagement significantly.

---

## Notable Insights
- Students assessed understanding differently than traditional metrics suggested
- Reading assignments became boring quickly for most children
- Questions engaged students meaningfully
- Arcade-style games failed to sustain engagement as designed
- "The feeling of learning is key" (presented at SXSW)
