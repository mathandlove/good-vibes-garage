# Understanding the Emotional Experience of Digital Math Lessons

**Source URL:** https://www.buildempathy.com/casestudies/digitalmath  
**Scraped:** April 2026

---

## Study Overview

The math team developed a working prototype for teaching addition (double and single digits). mPath evaluated emotional responses from 19 children during the learning process.

**Key Outcome:** "Our results helped the team understand how classroom pedagogy does not work when children are learning by themselves digitally."

---

## Three Major Findings

### Animations
- **Before:** Long animation clips with movement, jokes, and instruction designed to teach like teachers
- **After:** Animations should only serve as short introductions; after approximately five seconds, animations disengaged children and caused them to miss key instructions

### Feedback
- **Before:** Minimal feedback ("That's not right; try again!") with answers provided after three failed attempts
- **After:** Dynamic feedback became the primary teaching method, helping children learn from mistakes and preventing "helplessness" or "click-to-continue" behaviors

### Teaching Interfaces
- **Before:** Interfaces were not directly taught; children struggled independently and interpreted interface difficulties as math inability
- **After:** Teaching interface usage is integral to teaching math content; modeling behavior with every new button (example: "+10 button" instruction)

## Methodology
- Children wore eye tracking glasses and MOXO Sensors
- Researchers created digital paper prototypes
- Testing revealed that answering questions was highly engaging
- Finding led to framework: "The Feeling of Learning" as engagement backbone
- Discovery: Educators should add scaffolding and support rather than adjusting content difficulty
- When UX improved and children learned tool usage, they simultaneously mastered the math concept
