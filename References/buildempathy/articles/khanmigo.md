## Full Article Content

The author argues that AI tutoring tools like Khanmigo will primarily benefit already-privileged students while failing underrepresented learners. Hedman's central premise: these tools require students to voluntarily seek help, a behavior uncommon among disadvantaged youth.

### Key observations from his teaching experience:
- Students at Boys & Girls Club (25% reading proficiency) avoid using Khanmigo voluntarily
- Many learners treat education as guessing games rather than skill-building
- Help-seeking requires three prerequisites: desire to learn, confidence to acknowledge gaps, and ability to access external resources

The author identifies a fundamental GenAI limitation: these systems only serve "those who are already internally motivated with strong self-efficacy."

### Proposed solutions:
1. Redesign assignments to increase motivation through elements like competition
2. Develop student self-efficacy by teaching learning processes before content

Hedman envisions classrooms shifting from drill-based testing toward student-directed inquiry, though acknowledges this requires systemic changes beyond technological innovation.
