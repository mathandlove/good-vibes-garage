Elliott’s Interview Responses

1) A specific moment where you watched someone struggle to build or create something AI could have helped them do

I have a variety of answers to this one. When I started, I did not even realize it was something I cared about. My good friend Kristen, she is a lawyer, but she is homebound now because she has some neurological challenges. And she did not want to be a lawyer anymore. So I showed her how to start using Claude Code. She actually built a tic-tac-toe puzzle game that her grandfather had envisioned and I had drawn out and wanted to make into code. And she actually used Claude Code to do it.

But what was interesting to me was it was not just a simple, okay, I open it up and say make a game. I actually had to teach her a bunch of skills, like how to debug, how to reflect, how to set up specs. And also the more important piece was showing her that she could do it and to believe in herself and to try it out and not just buy into this idea of, oh, it just does not work.

Follow-up: How did her mindset shift?

She started with, this is crazy, there is no way I can do it, to this is fun, to, oh, this is actually a skill I have and I can actually build things now. She now actually runs her own little mini company where people hire her to use Claude Code to build things for them.

Follow-up: Was that part of what planted the seed for Good Vibe Garage?

It was. And I noticed there were a bunch of other teachers that I started to visit at South by Southwest who had kind of the same thing. They are builders, they want to make things, but they are really hesitant to dive in. And then they dive into things like Lovable or Replit, which are easier to use, but Claude Code is not hard to use. And because it is easier, it is making it so that they kind of get stuck and then they cannot figure out how to go forward.

Follow-up: What do you do differently to help people not stay stuck?

There are a few things that I do. One is that vibe coding, though it seems as simple as putting in text, has all these little random things you have to be good at. It is like, here is how you set up a web page correctly. Here is how you save it on GitHub. Here is how you post it on Vercel. Here is what to do in case it gets wrong. So there are all these little obstacles that are totally teachable and they do not really require programming, but if you do not get taught them, it is going to feel overwhelming.

Follow-up: How do you know they have crossed the threshold?

When I check in with them one or two weeks later, they have already built out the entire webpage.

2) What were you doing right before Good Vibe Garage became an idea? What was not working about that chapter?

Before Good Vibe Garage, I had a website called BuildEmpathy.com. I ran a consulting company called Empath. And under all of it is a deep, deep passion for iterative design. We build things fast, we test them with users, and we keep that iterative process going so that innovation can flourish through rapid prototyping.

I still have that passion, but what I am finding is that most people feel really uncomfortable doing that. So it is still something I want to deeply share, but what I notice is that people are much more open to building out and using the tools themselves to build things, aka teaching them to vibe code, than hiring someone like me for a lot of money to build out a prototype.

⸻

3) Growing up, what did making things mean to you? Was there a version of “the garage” in your childhood?

My mother was a really creative person, but our vision of creativity was very construction paper and glue. I remember there was this book at the library. It was my favorite book. It was How to Build Spaceships and Aliens, and it had an alien with a walnut head and googly eyes that you could put together, and I thought that was the most amazing thing.

So I always naturally had a desire to make things. And it took me all the way until MIT to really feel like, oh, I can be a builder too. It is not just for the artists.

⸻

4) Who told you, explicitly or implicitly, that building things was not for certain kinds of people?

I do not think anyone told me I could not build, but I remember in my drawing class in fifth grade, there was a really good artist, and he made the coolest drawings. So whenever I looked at him, I was like, I cannot draw because I cannot make drawings nearly as well as David can.

And so when our skills do not meet what we want to produce, we kind of lose the ability to actually build and create. What is interesting about AI is it kind of flips the switch, and now AI can help you build things that you cannot even fully imagine yourself, which really makes it feel like, oh, then I might be able to do this.

⸻

5) When did you first feel what you are now calling “the right mindset”?

For me, I am a good coder, but it has always been too slow, and I do not spend enough time coding to be able to make things fast. So oftentimes I would have to hire coders or pray that we did not have to do too complicated coding with my clients.

But it was probably when I was working with Titleist Can Read. They needed a prototype in a week. We only had a week to do research, build out a prototype, and test it. But then with AI, I was able to make their concept in less than one day, and we could test it the next day, and then within a week, we knew what was working and what was not.

That was probably the first moment I was like, oh, I am no longer constrained to, that is a good idea but we cannot build it. We can literally build it right now and test it out immediately.

⸻

6) Describe the person who has the dream but has not built it yet. What story are they telling themselves?

Let us take, for example, the person I want to work with this week, Angela Dino. She is a professor in anthropology, has no coding background, but she teaches the honors engineering program.

For her, she is really struggling because she wants to use AI to help her with grading, but she does not get AI. She does not know what AI can do, and she does not know how to make it happen. So what I am hoping to do with her in 30 minutes is show her, okay, all you have to do is tell AI this is my vision and my goal, and it will make something for you. And we can actually watch that process unfold together.

7) What do people in the AI space get catastrophically wrong?

If you listen to people in computer science programs or AI people, it is like, what do we need to know to understand AI? They are like, they need to understand the basics of machine learning. And that is not true at all.

In fact, I know the basics of machine learning and it does not really help me day to day at all on how to use AI. What people really need is a mind shift. They need to be able to actually think through a problem and describe that problem and paint that vision very vividly so that they can get to the end.

12) Who are you not making this for? Who would you actively want to turn away?

I am not making this for the vast majority of people who are already vibe coding. Vibe coding right now is replacing engineering. So I know lots of people who just use AI to write all their code for them, and they just have a bunch of bots running every day. There is a person on our team who, that is all he does, is manage his bots well.

Those people are doing great. They do not need my help because they are already making things all the time. In fact, I will be antithetical to them because I will be arguing for mindset over the latest technology and the latest tools, which is what they care most about.

⸻

13) What happens to the world if nobody does what Good Vibe Garage is trying to do?

I think there are other people doing this, but what is happening right now, I put a post about this on LinkedIn. You know how many people posted things about their April Fool’s jokes? No one. No one made any April Fool’s jokes with vibe coding, which I did. I made one, so at least one person did. But I found that very fascinating.

Because April Fool’s jokes are things that normally you would not be able to make stuff for, but now you can make replicated websites, you can make funny images, you can do so many things, but people do not have that builder mindset.

So what is happening here is that we have these amazing tools so that we can innovate and share ideas very quickly, like within 30 minutes. But it does not matter if you do not have a builder mindset. What will happen in the future is that we will have this great promise of AI tools, but it will not matter because the only people who are innovating are the people who already knew how to innovate pre-AI. And so we will be stuck in the day to day.

Additional Builder Mindset Responses

What is the first belief that changes when someone shifts into a builder mindset?

There is a sense of ownership. There is not a sense of the world makes for me, but I can make for the world. There is a sense of creativity unleashed, the idea that I can build what I am envisioning, and there is a sense of asking not whether it is possible, but how can I do it, and an excitement around building it. And there is a sense of exploration and creativity as well.

Reflection on your role in teaching builder mindset

I like to call myself out on this. For the longest time, my old consulting company Empath, I did not really help people have a builder mindset. It is just that I have a really, really strong builder mindset myself. So this teaching people a builder mindset is kind of a new space for me. So I do not want to say I am an expert and I have helped a bunch of people have this.

And I do not even know if helping other people is the thing I care most about. It is more demonstrating it and showing that it is possible and how to do it, and getting people excited about it.

What feels like proof of what you mean by builder mindset?

It almost happened at South by Southwest. A woman had a webpage that she wanted to make better, and so I just took her webpage into Claude Code and made it better in like 20 minutes.

So I think for me, there is a thing about building relationships with people who want to have a builder mindset. It is like meeting those people who have visions and helping them get to the end of that road. So it is not just me building my own stuff, but it is actually working with other people’s visions and building their pieces. That is more fun to me than just building my own apps right now.
